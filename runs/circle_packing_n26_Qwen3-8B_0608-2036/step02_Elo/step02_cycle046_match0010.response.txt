This approach has limited flexibility because the positions of the circles are pre-determined and not allowed to vary. While it may yield decent results, it does not exploit the full problem structure by allowing both centers and radii to be optimized. The optimization is constrained to a rigid grid, which may prevent the system from achieving higher "sum of radii" due to suboptimal center placement. The headroom for improvement is limited, as the grid structure is fixed and not adaptive.

**Program 2** allows both the centers and radii of the circles to be optimized simultaneously by flattening them into a single optimization vector. This approach is more flexible and better exploits the problem structure by treating all variables as part of the same optimization space. The use of a hexagonal grid as an initial guess is still present, but the program does not rigidly fix the centers, allowing for more potential improvements. The formulation of constraints and the optimization setup are also more robust and flexible, which enhances the likelihood of finding better solutions. The headroom for improvement is significantly higher, as the system is not constrained to a fixed grid and can explore a larger solution space.
